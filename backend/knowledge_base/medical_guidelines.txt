DIABETES MANAGEMENT — BLOOD GLUCOSE TARGETS
Fasting blood glucose target for adults with Type 2 Diabetes: 80–130 mg/dL (ADA 2024).
2-hour postprandial target: < 180 mg/dL.
HbA1c goal: < 7.0% for most non-pregnant adults; < 8.0% for patients with history of severe hypoglycemia, limited life expectancy, or extensive comorbidities.
Hypoglycemia is defined as blood glucose < 70 mg/dL (Level 1) and < 54 mg/dL (Level 2, clinically significant).
Hyperglycemic crisis threshold: blood glucose > 250 mg/dL warrants immediate clinical review.
Post-operative diabetic patients should maintain blood glucose 140–180 mg/dL in hospital to reduce infection risk and promote wound healing.

DIABETES — DIETARY RECOMMENDATIONS
Recommended foods: non-starchy vegetables (broccoli, spinach, kale), legumes (lentils, chickpeas), whole grains (oats, quinoa, barley), lean proteins (chicken, turkey, tofu), fatty fish (salmon, sardines), avocados, berries, nuts (walnuts, almonds), olive oil.
Foods to limit or avoid: refined carbohydrates (white bread, white rice), sugary beverages (soda, fruit juice), processed snacks (chips, cookies), full-fat dairy in excess, red and processed meats, foods with added sugar.
Consistent carbohydrate intake across meals reduces glycemic variability. The Mediterranean diet pattern is associated with improved HbA1c and cardiovascular outcomes in diabetic patients.
Patients on insulin should time carbohydrate intake with their injection schedule. Alcohol can cause delayed hypoglycemia and should be consumed with food if at all.

HYPERTENSION — BLOOD PRESSURE CLASSIFICATION
Normal: Systolic < 120 mmHg AND Diastolic < 80 mmHg.
Elevated: Systolic 120–129 mmHg AND Diastolic < 80 mmHg.
Stage 1 Hypertension: Systolic 130–139 mmHg OR Diastolic 80–89 mmHg.
Stage 2 Hypertension: Systolic ≥ 140 mmHg OR Diastolic ≥ 90 mmHg.
Hypertensive Crisis: Systolic > 180 mmHg AND/OR Diastolic > 120 mmHg — requires immediate medical evaluation.
Treatment target for most adults with hypertension: < 130/80 mmHg (ACC/AHA 2023).
For patients > 65 years, target systolic 125–135 mmHg balancing cardiovascular benefit against orthostatic hypotension risk.

---CHUNK---
HYPERTENSION — LIFESTYLE AND DIETARY INTERVENTIONS
The DASH diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension) reduces systolic BP by 8–14 mmHg.
Sodium restriction to < 2,300 mg/day (ideally < 1,500 mg/day) lowers BP by 5–6 mmHg systolic.
Weight reduction: every 1 kg of body weight lost reduces systolic BP by ~1 mmHg.
Aerobic exercise (150 minutes/week of moderate intensity) reduces BP by 5–8 mmHg.
Limiting alcohol to ≤ 1 drink/day (women) or ≤ 2 drinks/day (men) lowers BP by 4 mmHg.
Potassium-rich foods (bananas, sweet potatoes, avocado, spinach) counteract sodium's BP-raising effects.
Caffeine consumption can acutely raise BP by 3–4 mmHg; chronic effects in habitual consumers are minimal.

HEART RATE — CLINICAL INTERPRETATION
Normal resting heart rate (adults): 60–100 bpm.
Bradycardia: resting heart rate < 60 bpm. Symptomatic bradycardia (dizziness, syncope, fatigue) requires investigation.
Tachycardia: resting heart rate > 100 bpm. Persistent tachycardia at rest may indicate pain, anxiety, fever, dehydration, anemia, hyperthyroidism, or cardiac arrhythmia.
Target heart rate during moderate aerobic exercise: 50–70% of maximum HR (220 − age).
Post-operative patients: resting HR > 100 bpm for > 24 hours should be evaluated for infection, pulmonary embolism, or fluid deficit.
Athletes and highly conditioned individuals may have a resting HR of 40–60 bpm as a normal variant.

OXYGEN SATURATION (SpO2) — CLINICAL THRESHOLDS
Normal SpO2: 95–100%.
Acceptable lower limit in patients with COPD or chronic hypoxemia: 88–92% (higher targets may suppress hypoxic drive).
SpO2 < 94%: supplemental oxygen is generally indicated.
SpO2 < 90%: clinically significant hypoxemia; consider urgent intervention.
SpO2 < 85%: life-threatening; immediate emergency response required.
Pulse oximetry accuracy is reduced by nail polish, poor perfusion, motion artifact, and darker skin pigmentation (may read 2–4% higher than true value in some devices).
Persistent SpO2 < 94% in a post-operative patient despite supplemental oxygen indicates possible respiratory complication (atelectasis, pneumonia, PE).

BODY TEMPERATURE — FEVER AND HYPOTHERMIA
Normal body temperature range: 36.1–37.2°C (97.0–99.0°F).
Low-grade fever: 37.3–38.0°C.
Fever: ≥ 38.0°C (100.4°F). In post-operative patients, fever in the first 24–48 hours is often atelectasis; after 48 hours, suspect infection.
High fever: ≥ 39.5°C requires investigation and antipyretic treatment.
Hyperthermia > 41°C: medical emergency — risk of seizures, organ damage.
Hypothermia: core temperature < 35°C. Mild (32–35°C), Moderate (28–32°C), Severe (< 28°C).
In elderly patients, baseline temperature is lower; a temperature of 37.5°C may represent significant fever in this population.
Post-surgical fever workup: the "5 W's" — Wind (atelectasis, pneumonia), Water (UTI), Wound (surgical site infection), Walking (DVT/PE), Wonder drugs (drug fever).

HEART DISEASE — POST-OPERATIVE RECOVERY GUIDELINES
Patients recovering from cardiac events or cardiac surgery should monitor resting HR and BP daily.
Cardiac rehabilitation programs reduce all-cause mortality by 20–25% and improve exercise tolerance.
Activity restrictions: avoid lifting > 4.5 kg for 6–8 weeks post-sternotomy. Begin with short walks (5–10 minutes) and gradually increase by 1–2 minutes per day.
Warning signs requiring immediate medical attention: chest pain or pressure, new shortness of breath at rest, palpitations, syncope, swelling in legs (possible DVT), fever > 38°C, wound redness or discharge.
Medications: strict adherence to antiplatelet therapy, beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors, and statins is critical; missed doses are associated with increased MACE (Major Adverse Cardiovascular Events) risk.
Dietary restriction: sodium < 2,000 mg/day; limit saturated fats; avoid high-cholesterol foods.

RESPIRATORY CONDITIONS — COPD AND RECOVERY
COPD severity (GOLD staging) is based on FEV1 as a percentage of predicted: GOLD 1 (≥ 80%), GOLD 2 (50–79%), GOLD 3 (30–49%), GOLD 4 (< 30%).
SpO2 target in COPD: 88–92% to avoid suppression of hypoxic respiratory drive.
COPD exacerbation warning signs: increased dyspnea, change in sputum color/volume, new or worsening wheeze, confusion or decreased consciousness.
Pursed-lip breathing and diaphragmatic breathing exercises improve airflow efficiency.
Pulmonary rehabilitation improves quality of life and exercise capacity in COPD (strong evidence, GOLD guidelines).
Smoking cessation is the single most effective intervention to slow COPD progression.
Patients should avoid cold air, strong smells, and air pollution which can trigger bronchospasm.

MEDICATION ADHERENCE — CLINICAL IMPACT
Medication non-adherence accounts for approximately 50% of treatment failures in chronic disease management.
A 10% improvement in medication adherence is associated with a 13% reduction in all-cause hospitalization in hypertensive patients.
In Type 2 Diabetes, consistent adherence to antidiabetic medications reduces HbA1c by 1.0–2.0% and reduces the risk of microvascular complications by 25–40%.
Factors affecting adherence: complex dosing schedules, side effects, cost, patient health literacy, forgetfulness.
Strategies to improve adherence: pill organizers, phone reminders, blister packs, once-daily formulations, motivational interviewing.
Missed doses of antihypertensives should NOT be doubled. If a dose is missed by > 12 hours, skip it and resume the next scheduled dose.
Healthcare providers should assess adherence at every visit using non-judgmental questioning (e.g., "Most people miss doses sometimes; how often do you miss yours?").

RISK STRATIFICATION — GENERAL PRINCIPLES
Low risk: all vitals within normal range, high medication adherence (> 90%), no recent alerts. Recommended monitoring: weekly check-ins.
Medium risk: one or more vitals approaching threshold, adherence 70–90%, occasional alerts. Recommended monitoring: every 2–3 days; review medications.
High risk: vitals at or beyond threshold, adherence < 70%, consecutive missed doses, multiple concurrent alerts. Recommended action: immediate physician review; consider hospitalization.
Composite risk indicators: a patient with high blood sugar + elevated BP + tachycardia simultaneously has a significantly elevated risk of acute cardiovascular event compared to any single abnormality.
Age > 65 and disease duration > 10 years are independent risk multipliers. Elderly patients with a single high-risk vital warrant the same urgency as middle-aged patients with two.

HYPERGLYCEMIC CRISIS — MANAGEMENT PROTOCOL
Blood sugar > 250 mg/dL: patient should check for ketones if Type 1 diabetic; increase water intake; avoid strenuous exercise; contact physician if values persist after 2 hours.
Blood sugar > 300 mg/dL: high risk of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) or hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state (HHS). Symptoms: extreme thirst, frequent urination, nausea, abdominal pain, fruity breath (DKA), confusion (HHS). Seek emergency care immediately.
Blood sugar > 400 mg/dL: medical emergency regardless of symptoms. Call emergency services.
Insulin correction doses ("correction bolus"): only administer if prescribed; never guess a dose. Correction factor is typically (current glucose – target glucose) / insulin sensitivity factor.
Common triggers of hyperglycemia: infection/illness, missed insulin or oral medication, dietary indiscretion, physical or emotional stress, steroid medications.
Action plan if blood sugar > 250 after 2 hours: check for illness, ensure medication was taken, drink 250 mL of water, recheck in 1 hour. If still > 250, call physician.

HYPOGLYCEMIA — RECOGNITION AND TREATMENT
Mild hypoglycemia (blood sugar 54–70 mg/dL): administer the "Rule of 15" — consume 15 g of fast-acting carbohydrate (4 glucose tablets, 120 mL fruit juice, or 3 teaspoons of sugar). Recheck in 15 minutes; repeat if still < 70 mg/dL.
Moderate hypoglycemia (blood sugar < 54 mg/dL): treat with 20–30 g fast-acting carbohydrates. Recheck in 15 minutes. If conscious, avoid lying down. Do not consume fat-containing foods (chocolate, peanut butter) as they slow glucose absorption.
Severe hypoglycemia (< 40 mg/dL or unconscious patient): do not give anything by mouth. Use glucagon injection kit if available; call emergency services immediately. If no glucagon: rub glucose gel or honey inside the cheek carefully.
Post-hypoglycemia: eat a balanced snack with protein and complex carbohydrate after recovery (e.g., peanut butter on wholegrain crackers).
Nocturnal hypoglycemia risk factors: skipped bedtime snack, excessive exercise earlier in the day, alcohol consumption, long-acting insulin peaking overnight.
Patients on sulfonylureas or insulin who experience hypoglycemia should notify their physician for medication dose review.

BLOOD PRESSURE EMERGENCY — HYPERTENSIVE CRISIS PROTOCOL
Systolic > 180 mmHg OR diastolic > 120 mmHg with symptoms (chest pain, shortness of breath, severe headache, visual changes, confusion, neurological deficits): hypertensive emergency — call emergency services immediately.
Systolic > 180 mmHg OR diastolic > 120 mmHg without symptoms: hypertensive urgency — contact physician within 1 hour; sit quietly, avoid exertion or caffeine, take prescribed antihypertensive if due.
Do NOT attempt to lower BP too rapidly at home; overly rapid reduction can cause stroke, MI, or renal failure. Target reduction of 25% in the first hour under physician supervision only.
Common causes of acute BP spike: missed medications, excessive sodium intake, pain, anxiety, stimulants (decongestants, energy drinks, cocaine), sleep deprivation.
Home BP cuff technique: rest 5 minutes before measurement; sit with feet flat, arm at heart level, bladder emptied. Take 2–3 readings 1 minute apart and average them.

HEART RATE ABNORMALITIES — CLINICAL ACTION GUIDE
Resting HR > 120 bpm in a non-exercising patient: evaluate for fever, dehydration, pain, anemia, anxiety, thyroid dysfunction, or arrhythmia. Check temperature and hydration status first.
Resting HR > 150 bpm: high suspicion for supraventricular tachycardia (SVT) or atrial fibrillation; seek immediate medical evaluation.
HR < 50 bpm with symptoms (dizziness, presyncope, fatigue): evaluate for complete heart block, sick sinus syndrome, or medication toxicity (beta-blockers, digoxin overdose).
Irregular pulse: may indicate atrial fibrillation; confirm with ECG. AF increases stroke risk 5-fold; anticoagulation therapy is critical.
Exercise-induced HR > 90% of maximum (220 − age) in patients with known heart disease: stop exercise; rest; notify physician.
Palpitations with chest discomfort, syncope, or near-syncope: treat as cardiac emergency until proven otherwise.

OBESITY AND METABOLIC SYNDROME — MONITORING AND MANAGEMENT
Metabolic syndrome is defined by 3 or more: waist circumference > 102 cm (men) or > 88 cm (women), triglycerides ≥ 150 mg/dL, HDL < 40 mg/dL (men) or < 50 mg/dL (women), BP ≥ 130/85 mmHg, fasting glucose ≥ 100 mg/dL.
Weight loss of 5–10% of body weight significantly improves blood sugar, BP, and lipid profile in overweight patients with metabolic syndrome.
Caloric deficit of 500–750 kcal/day produces 0.5–0.75 kg/week weight loss — a sustainable and clinically recommended rate.
Physical activity target: 150–300 minutes/week moderate-intensity aerobic activity plus 2 days/week resistance training.
Sleep apnea is strongly associated with metabolic syndrome and worsens blood sugar control, BP, and cardiovascular risk; patients should be screened if symptomatic (snoring, daytime somnolence).
Bariatric surgery is considered when BMI ≥ 40, or ≥ 35 with obesity-related comorbidities, and lifestyle interventions have failed.

CHRONIC KIDNEY DISEASE (CKD) — MONITORING IN DIABETIC AND HYPERTENSIVE PATIENTS
CKD staging (eGFR): G1 (≥ 90), G2 (60–89), G3a (45–59), G3b (30–44), G4 (15–29), G5 (< 15, kidney failure).
Blood pressure target in CKD with proteinuria: < 130/80 mmHg using ACE inhibitor or ARB as first-line agent.
Diabetes + CKD: SGLT2 inhibitors (e.g., empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) provide renal-protective benefits beyond glucose lowering and are recommended in CKD stages G2–G3.
Metformin: reduce dose when eGFR < 45; discontinue when eGFR < 30 due to risk of lactic acidosis.
Monitor potassium closely in patients on ACE inhibitors/ARBs with CKD — hyperkalemia risk. Avoid NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) in all CKD patients.
Annual monitoring: urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR), serum creatinine, eGFR, potassium, hemoglobin (anemia common in CKD stages 3–5).

MENTAL HEALTH AND CHRONIC DISEASE — INTEGRATED CARE
Depression affects 20–30% of patients with chronic conditions such as diabetes, heart failure, and COPD; it significantly worsens adherence and clinical outcomes.
PHQ-2 screening questions: "Over the past 2 weeks, have you felt little interest or pleasure in doing things?" and "Have you felt down, depressed, or hopeless?" Positive screen warrants PHQ-9 assessment.
Diabetes distress is distinct from clinical depression and is driven by fear of hypoglycemia, burden of self-management, and worry about complications. Validate and address in consultations.
Anxiety disorders are independently associated with worse blood pressure control and increased cardiac events.
Collaborative care models (integrating mental health within primary care) improve glycemic control and reduce depressive symptoms in diabetic patients.
Patients with high risk scores and low adherence should be assessed for depression, anxiety, and social support barriers before escalating medication doses.
